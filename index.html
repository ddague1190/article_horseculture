On horses in the Plains

Before the horses

From 10,000 years ago to approximately 1100, the Great Plains were only sparsely inhabitated by humans. Groups were small - with at most a few dozen individuals living together. They subsisted on mixed hunting and gathering wild plants.

Corn was introduced into the area around the year 700. Little by little, groups began to shift from foraging to farming and were living in permanent settlments. By about 1250, most Plains resisdents had made this shift. Without horses, travel and chasing game were more difficult and risky ventures. They lived in large-earth berm structures as opposed to tipis or other less permanent dwellings. They refer to these communities as Plains Village cultures.

It was in this period that the first European explorers made contact with the Plains people. In the Coronado Expedition, we have an account of areas spanning current-day Mexico through Kansas in year 1540. Coronado was searching for a mythical Seven Cities of Gold - which would have been somewhere in Kansas had it existed. He found rolling and fertile hills which produced fine corn, pumpkins, and tobacco. The only gold he found was from one Native person. He guessed - likely in humor - that the source of this gold could only have been from one of his own men.

The Last Conquistador brought many horses

The Coronado Expedition only brought 16 horses and only 2 of these were mares. Therefore Coronado's horses are unlikely to be the progenitors of what were to become the cornerstone of the Plains Horse Culture. It was instead Juan de OÃ±ate's cargo of some several hundred horses that provided this bolus. He arrived from Spain in 1592 to renew the search for the Seven Cities of Gold and to establish a colony. His impacts on the Native people were more devestating and his conduct so ruthless that he was admonished and exiled from the region.

The Spanish kept many Native people as slaves. Though they attempted to guard their knowledge of riding, some of the captives would flee their captors - and take some of the horses with them. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 was the largest such event. The Plains Indians also got their horses through trade and through finding horses that had escaped on their own.

A fully developed Horse Culture

The Commanches - whom for all this time had remained a hunter-gatherer nomadic society - were the first to leap headfirst into a purely nomadic, bison-hunting existence. They dominated the Southern plains and displaced many of the farming and mixed-economy settlements. The milder winters meant that each family could afford more horses. This resulted in a stratification of classes within their culture. Moreover, overgrazing by the horses had deleterious effects on the ecosystem.

The success of the Commanches encouraged other tribes to adopt the nomad lifestyle. But the horses thrived less in colder climates into the Northerm Plains and up into Canada. Residents of these lands hunted bison on foot and remained more dependent on dogs for carrying goods. They were also forced to desperate acts such as stealing horses from neighboring tribes.

The Lakota or Teton Sioux (think 'Dances with Wolves') enjoyed a happy medium between the North and the South. Their herd sizes were smaller but still more than enough. (For reference, 6 or 7 horses would have plentifully secured the transportation needs of a large family). It also helped that the Lakota and Sioux occupied prime Bison territory.

Ascertaining the quality of the relationship with the horse

First and foremost, the horses were symbols of power and wealth for the Plains Indians whose culture they transformed. They were not mearly a tool or possession. Then again, physical elements which were life-sustaining were in general eleveted to a spiritual plane by Plains folk.
